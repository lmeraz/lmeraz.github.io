Let's face it. No one has a perfect childhood. We are a product of our elements. We are each a beautiful art piece put together with all means available to us at the time of creation. It's great to reflect on what the art of us has to say. 

It's taken an open mind and curiosity about my childhood to see it's real value. Though it was not perfect, I made the most of it. Now, older in age, I see myself performing acts of kindness to myself. A past me is sure as hell proud of where I am today. 

Below is my plan to be a good parent myself.

## Manage Your Baggage
It's important to know what you are dealing with yourself. Understand that it's your job to have a positive relationship with your self. Your self is the only perspective you have available. You can change your perspective on your circumstances and take responsibility for what's ahead. It's unfair to expose a child, new to this world, to your baggage. With so much potential ahead, why should they carry your weight?

## Don't Confuse Chronic Unpredictable Toxic Stress with Childhood Challenges that Foster Resilience
Children need a safe environment to explore their surroundings and take risks. When you don't make any sense, they suffer. They don't know how to react. They may learn to cope in an unhealthy or unproductive way. Don't let that happen.

## Instill the 4 S's in your children
The 4 S's are Safety, Security, Seen, and Soothe. These 4 S's help parents create loving bonds with their children.
Being safe requires physical and emotional safety. Use soft tones of voice, and 'I' statements to bring up difficult topics. When you don't feel safe, you'll fight, flee, or withdraw. The same happens to a kind. You can also increase the feeling of safety by being vulnerable. 

Security is a sense of safety combined with stability. Though there might be ebbs and flows in your relationship, you know that there's a consistency and soon things will be back to normal. If you act on extremes, it leaves very little security for children. You can resolve arguments calmly. You and your child will both be thankful.

Children need to feel seen by their parents. It helps with building the child's sense of self. Remember, they just started life, you may be able to give them insights to feelings and emotions they've yet to identify or know the name of for that matter. Empathize with children. If you're having a hard time empathizing, mirroring back, or paraphrasing what your child is saying to you will is more than enough.

In soothing, it's essential to make positive physical contact every day to soothe each other. Not much changes from us as babies, crying and waiting for an adult to relieve our discomfort. 

## Look into Your Child's Eye
You communicate so many non-verbals through the eyes. Without full communication, you may make assumptions that are not true.  Eyes cast a massive amount of emotion. Never leave it to guesswork. Reveal what your eyes have to say.

## If you lose it, apologize right away
Sure there will be times when mindfulness goes out the window. It's important to own up to our errors. Take responsibility for your mistakes. Apologize. Be an adult, you know better.

## Validate and Normalize all of your child's emotions
All emotions are valid emotions if expressed by a human being. You cannot deprive someone of how they feel. The buddha says everyone suffers, and it's true. Sure, there are varying degrees of suffering. You may disagree with ones' perspective on what pain entails, but you should not heed judgment. Emotions are valid.

## Amplify the Good Feelings
You may dwell on negative emotions and feelings. How often do you celebrate the happy and pleasant moments? It's time to focus on the good and then reach out for those types of moments again.

## Stop, look, go
When an emotion arises, stop what you are doing. Look at what led to that emotion. Then act. You'll come to appreciate this time to buffer reaction into clarity.

## Give a Name to Difficult Emotions
What name to give that feeling that you don't quite understand? Giving the unknown a known name eases confusion and settles uncertainty in expressing emotions. Use a full dictionary of emotions to identify and explore the feelings you feel. It will help when dealing with other's feelings.

## The Incredible Power of the Twenty-Second Hug
We are social creatures. All of us, to some degree, express ourselves with touch. One of the most effective tools in sharing our empathy with someone is a hug. A hug provides nurture and protection. Twenty seconds seem too long on the giving end? The receiving end will appreciate every second in the sheltering comfort of your arms.

## Make "What's Happening" a Safe and Open Conversation
You need to have conversations with no judgment attached. If there are any preconceived notions, you can guarantee the truth will never come out. Don't make the environment for discussion a hostile one. Be prepared with an open mind. Throw assumptions and expectations out. Trust that communication free of judgment leads to great things.

## A Child Needs a Reliable Adult  or Mentor
Children look to their elders to borrow and gain wisdom. You can help shape their path, saving them from committing your own mistakes and leading them to greener pastures. You may not have all the answers. Be honest and share that. Provide them with guidance on where they may find the answer.

## Bring Mindfulness to Schools
Mindfulness doesn't need to stay at home. Foster it in school and other activities. It's an everyday commitment to a healthy habit.
